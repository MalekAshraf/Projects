Upbringing & Education

Ahmed Hassan Zewail (February 26, 1946 - August 2, 2016) is an Egyptian-American chemist who won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1999 for his research in the field of femtochemistry. He invented a microscope that photographs laser beams in a femtosecond time frame, and thus molecules can be seen during chemical reactions. He is considered the pioneer of femtochemistry, and is called the “Father of Femtochemistry.” He is a professor of chemistry and a professor of physics at the California Institute of Technology. He died in 2016.

Ahmed Hassan Zewail was born on February 26, 1946 in the city of Damanhour, Beheira Governorate, and at the age of 4 years he moved with his family to the city of Desouk in Kafr El-Sheikh Governorate, where he grew up and received his primary and secondary education there. He joined the Faculty of Science at Alexandria University after graduating from high school. He obtained a Bachelor of Science degree with honors in 1967 in chemistry. He worked as a teaching assistant at the college, then obtained a master’s degree for research in the science of light.

He traveled to the United States to continue his studies on a scholarship, and obtained a doctorate from the University of Pennsylvania in laser science. He then worked as a researcher at the University of California, Berkeley (1974-1976). Then he moved to work at the California Institute of Technology since 1976, which is one of the largest scientific universities in the United States of America. He obtained American citizenship in 1982. He rose through academic positions at Caltech University until he became a major professor of chemistry there, which is the highest university scientific position in the United States of America, succeeding Linus Pauling, who won the Nobel Prize twice. The first is in chemistry and the second is in world peace.
